What is BMI and How to Calculate It?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple measurement used to determine whether a person has a healthy body weight in relation to their height. It is widely used by healthcare professionals as a quick screening tool to identify potential weight-related health risks.

What Does BMI Stand For?

BMI stands for Body Mass Index. It is a numerical value derived from a person’s weight and height. Although it does not directly measure body fat, it provides a useful estimate for most adults.

How is BMI Calculated?

The formula for BMI is:

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height (m²)

For example, if a person weighs 70 kg and is 1.75 meters tall:

70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.86

This value falls within the normal weight range.

BMI Categories for Adults

  • Underweight: Below 18.5
  • Normal weight: 18.5 – 22.9
  • Overweight: 23 – 24.9
  • Obese: 25 and above

These ranges may vary slightly based on ethnicity and regional health guidelines.

Why is BMI Important?

BMI helps identify potential health risks associated with being underweight or overweight. Maintaining a healthy BMI may reduce the risk of:

  • Heart disease
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • High blood pressure
  • Joint problems
  • Metabolic disorders

Limitations of BMI

Although BMI is useful, it has limitations:

  • Does not measure body fat directly
  • May not be accurate for athletes
  • Does not consider muscle mass
  • Does not account for fat distribution

For a more complete health assessment, BMI should be considered alongside waist circumference, body fat percentage, and overall lifestyle habits.

BMI for Indian Adults

In many Asian populations, including India, health risks may begin at lower BMI levels. Therefore, slightly lower BMI cut-offs are often used to identify overweight and obesity.

How Often Should You Check BMI?

Checking BMI every few months is sufficient unless you are actively working on weight management. Monitoring changes over time can help track progress.

Final Thoughts

BMI is a quick and convenient way to assess weight-related health risk. While it is not a perfect measurement, it remains one of the most widely used tools worldwide.
